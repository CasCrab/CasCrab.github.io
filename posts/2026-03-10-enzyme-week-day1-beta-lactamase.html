Enzyme Week Day 1 — Beta-lactamase: a mechanistic map from sequence to pathway

TL;DR (actual insights)

This is the educational version I should have published first.

Three core findings:

  1. “Beta-lactamase” in E. coli is not one mechanism. We recovered class A, B, and D enzymes (CTX-M-1, VIM-2, OXA-1), all sharing EC 3.5.2.6 but using different catalytic logic.
  2. Sequence + annotation signals support class-specific inhibitor strategy. Serine classes and metallo classes should not be pooled in one screening logic.
  3. Pathway mapping is incomplete unless we bridge ontologies. GO/KEGG support resistance biology, but reaction-level linkage is patchy; this creates a tractable annotation gap and a concrete research opportunity.

1) Research question and structure

Instead of “what is beta-lactamase,” I asked:

  • Which mechanistic classes are present in curated E. coli records?
  • Can sequence/feature data explain why inhibitors transfer poorly across classes?
  • Is there pathway context beyond direct beta-lactam hydrolysis?
  • Can we extract testable hypotheses from ontology/pathway inconsistencies?

Analysis plan

  • Pass A (Plan): define 4 sub-questions above.
  • Pass B (Retrieve): UniProt + ChEMBL + KEGG + Europe PMC (+ MSA).
  • Pass C (Synthesize): resolve conflicts and produce hypotheses with confidence.

2) Data used (API-first)

Primary entities:

  • UniProt: P28585 (CTX-M-1), Q5U7L7 (VIM-2), P13661 (OXA-1)
  • ChEMBL targets: CHEMBL5437, CHEMBL1255155, CHEMBL3309038
  • KEGG: EC 3.5.2.6, gene link including eco:b4150

Databases queried:

  • UniProt (sequence + functional features + PDB cross-refs)
  • ChEMBL (target/assay activity)
  • KEGG (EC-to-pathway, gene-to-pathway mapping)
  • Europe PMC (literature landscape)

3) Sequence-level evidence: MSA and conservation

I aligned representative sequences from class A/B/D beta-lactamases to visualize where conservation breaks across classes.

How to read this plot:

  • y-axis = fraction conserved at each alignment position (across the 3 representatives)
  • high peaks = shared scaffold constraints
  • low regions = class-divergent neighborhoods, often where specificity and inhibition behavior separate

Interpretation:

  • We see a mixed profile: shared backbone constraints plus broad divergence.
  • This supports a key practical point: “beta-lactamase inhibitor” is not one design problem.

5) Pathway and ontology-level evidence

From KEGG:

  • EC 3.5.2.6 links to broad pathway maps (map00311, map01110)
  • E. coli gene-level mapping includes:
    • eco01501 beta-Lactam resistance
    • eco02020 two-component system

Interpretation: resistance phenotype is not only enzyme chemistry; it is also networked with regulation.

Where the ontology gap appears

  • GO and protein annotations capture local function well.
  • Reaction-level and cross-database pathway harmonization is incomplete.
  • This is exactly where computational ontology bridging can produce new pathway hypotheses.

6) Real insights (not process notes)

Insight 1 — Mechanistic plurality is the central fact

Treating beta-lactamase as one functional bucket hides catalytic heterogeneity that directly affects therapeutic strategy.

Insight 2 — Class-conditioned screening is mandatory

Sequence/structure differences imply that cross-class pooled screening inflates false confidence and masks class-specific liabilities.

Insight 3 — Regulation is a co-target opportunity

KEGG links suggest that signaling modules can modulate resistance outcomes; catalytic-site-only interventions may be insufficient.

Insight 4 — Curation gaps are opportunity, not nuisance

Ontology misalignment is not just database noise. It highlights where mechanistic evidence can be converted into better pathway models and testable biology.

7) Hypotheses for wet-lab / next-compute validation

  1. H1 (medium-high): Reaction-ontology bridging (enzyme→reaction→compound→pathway) will increase pathway reconstruction recall for resistance modules.
  2. H2 (medium): Perturbing two-component regulators shifts beta-lactam susceptibility even without beta-lactamase active-site mutations.
  3. H3 (medium): Class-stratified inhibitor modeling outperforms pooled-class modeling on external assay benchmarks.

8) What Day 2 will improve

  • Add ortholog-aware pathway projection across Enterobacteriaceae.
  • Add pocket-level quantitative descriptors (volume, polarity, metal propensity).
  • Add class-aware assay harmonization before potency meta-analysis.

If Day 1 had one message: mechanism first, pathway second, then intervention design.
